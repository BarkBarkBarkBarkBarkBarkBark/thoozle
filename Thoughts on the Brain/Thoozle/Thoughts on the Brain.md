B.  Why Brain, Why? (function)

## The brain helps animals navigate their environment. It detects information, predicts outcomes, and attempts to optimize behavior

i.  Detection / Encoding

    1.  Collects and categorizes information about the environment

    2.  Encoded as good or bad, fed into prediction machine

ii. Prediction / Simulation

    1.  Generates likely outcomes

        a.  Bayesian Brain

    2.  May be based on internal or external circumstance

iii. Action Selection / Motor control

     1.  Complex movements are encoded in the brain

         a.  Talking

         b.  Writing

         c.  Playing an Instrument

     2.  Average outcome is weighted by affect

## Original function is threat detection and resource acquisition

i.  Hindbrain

ii. Midbrain

iii. Forbrain

## Relevant phenomena are encoded with subjective feeling tags of good or bad

i.  Concepts are ranked by strength of the associated feeling

ii. Things that are very good (reward) or very bad (punishment) change behavior. Outcomes are either over or underestimated based on valence

iii. Human rumination compounds behavioral adjustments associated with good or bad experience

## Social animals can derive pleasure / pain from abstract events not directly related to survival

i.  These basic systems were likely co-opted from more archaic systems to make sense of an increasingly symbolic environment

C.  The Root of Dreaming (Thought)

## Perception

i.  Environmental events are encoded by the brain, this leads to perception

    1.  An acoustic series is perceived as a word

    2.  A visual array is perceived as a dog

ii. Patterns of perception are stored for later retrieval

    1.  This works similarly to an audio sampler, or a camera

        a.  The mechanism is different

        b.  The end result is the same, an image or word can be produced on demand

        c.  These patterns are strung together to create a meaningful sequence.

            i.  Ex. A dog running in a field, barking, and then catching a ball

                1.  Could you see it?

                2.  Could you hear it?

iii. The brain encodes audio frequencies and photons, but that\'s not what we perceive

     1.  Specific arrays cause concept

## What is a thought?

i.  It\'s the prediction of circumstance. (arising of a would-be sensory array)

    1.  The core of thought is the auto stimulation of the afferent sensory pathways, leading to the generation of a signal which is identical to that which would arise from a corresponding real world event

## Prediction is the archetypal thought. It is bound to the present moment. Its main function is action selection. It occurs in response to the environment

1.  To be more precise, it is a prediction of sensory consequences

2.  It manifests as a partial depolarization of a cortical column, which is compared to incoming sensory information from the thalamus

3.  The difference between the prediction and the actual sensory experience is predictive error, and the normal thing to do is to adjust either expectations or behavior

<!-- -->

iii. Examples

     1.  Someone throws a ball, and you catch it

     2.  It starts raining, and you run to close the top of your convertible

     3.  You're cooking dinner and you realize you don't have any onions

iv. We experience this all the time

    1.  We love when things are what we're expecting

        a.  The bus shows up right on time

        b.  The restaurant makes the soup just how you like it

        c.  Paycheck direct deposits on the 17th

    2.  We notice when something is different

        a.  Your friend got a new haircut

        b.  The speakers are busted so the song doesn\'t sound right

        c.  All your furniture is rearranged

    3.  EXAMPLE - hitting a huge bell with a hammer

        a.  We imagine it is going to be loud

        b.  We brace ourselves as if it is going to be loud

        c.  We are surprised when it does not go as expected,

            i.  In the case we are surprised that it does not go as expected, there is a mismatch between the prediction and the experience

                1.  We either say "that was weird" or we adjust our expectations

## The derivative type of thought is imagination. It is unbound from the present moment. Its main function is planning. 

i.  It too is a prediction of sensory consequences

ii. Mental activity can never be fully separated from the environment, but mental contents may be unrelated to environment

    1.  Imagining what you'll do over summer break

    2.  Thinking about something else during a conversation

    3.  Planning a dinner this weekend

iii. This type of thinking is not necessarily relevant to the present moment, because the brain is capable of simulating our environment

     1.  I.e. Dreams

iv. The process of holding a concept in mind is inherently creative, because the perception is not driven by the environment, but instead generated from an internal process

E.  Motivation and The Impossible Task

## Motivation has three distinct aspect, perceived effort, perceived chance of success, and perceived reward

i.  In school, tasks which required great energy we made easy is the reward, as well as the likelihood of reward, were both high

ii. Revised formula - Risk adjusted Reward / Effort

## Motivation is a dispositional state towards action, or goal driven behavior

i.  Animals are motivated to survive

ii. We can believe we are motivated, but not act on our motivation

iii. An interesting example is the addict during sobriety

     1.  They may feel an urge, or motivation, to use, but the action is suppressed

iv. Another interesting example is the motivation to exercise, or improve our life,

    1.  It\'s possible that we don\'t act on this motivation, because the perceived adversity is greater than the incentive

v.  Motivation is not synonymous with action

<!-- -->

c.  Colloquially, motivation has two meanings

    i.  The first refers to a level of activation, or drive

    ii. The other refers to underlying beliefs

d.  Intrinsic vs extrinsic motivation

    i.  Intrinsic motivation is when the value is derived from the action itself, such as eating or sex

    ii. Extrinsic motivation is when the value is derived from a product of the action, such as education, or cleaning the house

    iii. The difference between these two things is purely cognitive, and differs arbitrarily from person to person

    iv. For example running, for some there is intrinsic motivation, and for others the goal is weight loss

    v.  Intrinsic motivation is more activating that extrinsic motivation

e.  Conscious vs unconscious motivation

f.  There are two types of motivation, aversive and incentive

    i.  Incentive motivation is the drive to receive something good

    ii. Aversive motivation is the drive to avoid something bad

    iii. These motivations compete especially in the case of extrinsic motivation, when the act itself may present as difficult

         1.  For example, the immediate discomfort of doing homework or cleaning the house outweighs the perceived incentive

    iv. If aversion is greater than incentive, then the impossible task arises

g.  Liking something, and wanting it, are two separate motivational factors.

    i.  You can want something but not like it (addiction)

    ii. You can also like something without wanting it. For example, I do not always want to eat candy.

    iii. They are closely related under normal circumstances, but their relationship can be teased apart

    iv. Liking is the subjective feeling of pleasure derived from experiencing, or expecting, a thing. It is associated with an analgesic effect

    v.  Wanting something is the state of being motivated to do something, whether or not you are acting on it

h.  As animals, our body is constantly trying to preserve energy

i.  If a task seems too difficult, or like the payoff is low, then we will inherently be less motivated to do it

j.  If a task seems easy, and the payoff large, then we will be motivated to do it.

k.  Even if a task is hard, if there is a certain large payoff then we are going to want to do it

l.  The impossible task is a task for which aversion is greater than incentive

    i.  Perhaps the latent motivation is there, but the action is not favorable for one of two reasons

        1.  The effort to likelihood of success ratio is low

        2.  The effort to level or reward is low

    ii. There are three main factors that determine level of effort

        1.  Required effort

        2.  Liklihood of reward

        3.  Value of reward

        4.  This could be modeled as ((success rate x value)/cost)

        5.  High chance of success (1), high value (10), low cost (1)

            a.  (1x10)/1 = 10

        6.  Low chance of success (0.1) high value (10) low cost (1)

            a.  (.1x10)/1 = 1

        7.  Any value less than 1 is a loss of energy over time

        8.  This system is encoded by our brain

            a.  Probably these three factors are encoded in dopaminergic neurons, which act in a circuit

            b.  Effort could be reduced to calories

            c.  Likelihood of success is encoded in dopaminergic neurons

            d.  Value is encoded in dopaminergic neurons

        9.  It is important to realize that these are all perceived values, and are subject to both conscious and unconscious modulation)

    iii. After all this calculation is done, it is compared to other potential actions

         1.  If other actions have a high motivational value, such as addictive behaviors, compared to the necessary task, the one which requires less effort will be done

         2.  This takes advantage of the fact that the brain is always trying to reduce metabolic needs, and there is a false perception of value

         3.  The way it works with the formula is that humans have a tendency to overestimate the likelihood of both reward and punishment, so good things and bad things are both perceived as more likely

         4.  Further, humans have a negativity bias so bad things seem more likely to happen than similarly good things

             a.  From an evolutionary perspective this makes sense, because bad things can kill us, but good things can't

G.  How Drugs Work

## Drugs act on existing systems

i.  The reason that drugs work is because they bind to receptors that already exist

ii. A lot can be learned about existing systems through the study of drugs and their actions

iii. Drugs don\'t do anything that the body is not already doing to itself

<!-- -->

b.  Types of Drugs

    i.  Recreational Drugs (produce euphoria)

    ii. Therapeutic drugs (No euphoria,

c.  Physiology

    i.  Receptors

        1.  Excitatory

        2.  Inhibitory

        3.  Modular

    ii. Types of molecules

        1.  Agonist

        2.  Antagonist

        3.  Reuptake Inhibitor

        4.  Enzyme Inhibitor

d.  Only certain compounds produce a change in consciousness

    i.  There are many receptors, but the activation of a select few achieve an alteration in consciousness. Some drugs work very well but do not have psychoactive effects.

    ii. For a system involved in consciousness, there will presumably be a synthetic analogue which produces subjective change.

e.  Main Systems

    i.  Catecholamines (Dopamine & Norepinephrine)

        1.  Function

            a.  Catecholamines are involved in action selection and a subjective feeling of pleasure.

            b.  Their release conveys a sense of importance and relevance. Their release reinforces preceding behavior. In humans the reinforced behavior is probably the one that is perceived to be relevant.

        2.  Analogues

            a.  Amphetamine type drugs

            b.  Cocaine (reuptake inhibitor)

    ii. Serotonin

        1.  Function

            a.  Serotonin helps to provide subjective salience

            b.  Serotonin is critical for pavlovian fear conditioning.

                i.  Serotonin depletion attenuate fear conditioning.

        2.  Analogues

            a.  LSD

            b.  DMT

            c.  MDMA

    iii. Endorphins

         1.  Function

             a.  Endorphin release brings a subjective feeling of pleasure.

             b.  They are released in response to painful stimuli to raise the pain threshold.

         2.  Analogues

             a.  Opioids

    iv. GABA

        1.  Function

            a.  GABA is the main inhibitory neurotransmitter. Most of the neurons in the brain are inhibitory, the active neurons quiet down the rest of them.

        2.  Analogues

            a.  Alcohol

            b.  Barbiturates

    v.  Glutamate

        1.  Function

            a.  Glutamate is the primary excitatory neurotransmitter. It is involved in learning and association.

        2.  Analogues

            a.  Ketamine / PCP

    vi. Anandamide

        1.  Function

            a.  Anandamide is naturally released after exercise. It signals to the body that it is time to rest and eat.

        2.  Analogues

            a.  Marijuana

    vii. Acetylcholine

         1.  Function

             a.  AcH is involved in every muscle contraction. It is a key neurotransmitter in the musculoskeletal system

         2.  Analogues

             a.  Nicotine
