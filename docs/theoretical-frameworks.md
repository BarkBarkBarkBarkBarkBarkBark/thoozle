## Theoretical Frameworks — The Models Behind the Claims

This brief draws on a number of established theories and models from neuroscience, psychology, and behavioral economics. None of these is the final word — science is an ongoing argument — but each represents a well-developed framework with substantial evidence behind it. They're listed here so you can evaluate the claims for yourself and pursue them further.

---

### Predictive Processing / Free Energy Principle
**Originators:** Karl Friston (free energy principle); Andy Clark (predictive processing framework)
**Used in:** Introduction, Chapter 2

The idea that the brain is fundamentally a prediction machine. Rather than passively receiving sensory input, the brain constantly generates expectations about what it *should* be perceiving and compares those expectations against what arrives. Mismatches — *prediction errors* — drive learning and attention. Friston's mathematical formulation (the free energy principle) proposes that all brain function can be understood as minimizing prediction error. Clark's work translates this into a broader cognitive framework. This is arguably the most influential theoretical paradigm in 21st-century neuroscience, though it remains debated — particularly regarding whether it's a testable theory or an unfalsifiable framework.

→ *Referenced when discussing: the detect-predict-act loop, prediction error, why surprises grab attention, why dreams are vivid.*

### Hebbian Learning
**Originator:** Donald Hebb (1949)
**Used in:** Chapter 1

"Neurons that fire together wire together." Hebb proposed that when two neurons are repeatedly active at the same time, the connection between them strengthens. This principle — now understood through mechanisms like *long-term potentiation* (LTP) and *spike-timing dependent plasticity* (STDP) — is the foundation of associative learning and memory. The actual neuroscience is more nuanced than Hebb's original formulation (timing, not just co-occurrence, matters), but the core insight holds.

→ *Referenced when discussing: the Memory Palace, associative memory, why things experienced together become linked.*

### Triune Brain Theory
**Originator:** Paul MacLean (1960s)
**Used in:** Introduction

The model of the brain as three nested layers — reptilian (hindbrain), paleomammalian (limbic/midbrain), and neomammalian (cortex). MacLean's model was hugely influential in popular science and is still a useful *pedagogical* tool, but comparative neuroscience has moved on. The brain did not evolve by simply wrapping new layers around old ones; all vertebrates have versions of all three regions. The model is used in this brief as a teaching scaffold, not as evolutionary fact.

→ *Referenced when discussing: the three-layer architecture, the mapping of brain regions to thinking/feeling/doing.*

---

### James-Lange Theory of Emotion / Somatic Marker Hypothesis
**Originators:** William James & Carl Lange (1880s); Antonio Damasio (somatic marker hypothesis, 1994)
**Used in:** Chapter 3

The James-Lange theory proposed that emotions are the brain's *interpretation* of bodily states — you don't cry because you're sad; you're sad because you cry. Damasio's somatic marker hypothesis updated this: the brain uses body-state signals ("somatic markers") to guide decision-making, especially under uncertainty. Emotional "gut feelings" are the conscious readout of these markers. This is well-supported but not the only theory of emotion — see Constructionist Theory below.

→ *Referenced when discussing: emotions as after-the-fact interpretations of bodily states, interoception, the physical nature of emotions.*

---

### Constructionist Theory of Emotion
**Originator:** Lisa Feldman Barrett (2006–present)
**Used in:** Chapter 3 (as counterpoint)

Barrett argues that emotions are not pre-wired biological programs (like "fear circuits" or "anger circuits") but are *constructed* by the brain from more basic ingredients: interoceptive signals, past experience, and conceptual knowledge. On this view, the brain doesn't detect "fear" — it constructs an *instance* of fear by interpreting ambiguous body signals in context. This theory challenges the classical view (that emotions are innate, universal categories) and has generated productive debate.

→ *Referenced when discussing: the caveat that not all researchers agree on the body-first model of emotion.*

### Circumplex Model of Affect
**Originator:** James Russell (1980)
**Used in:** Chapter 3

Russell proposed mapping emotions along two continuous dimensions — *valence* (good/bad) and *arousal* (high/low energy) — rather than treating them as discrete categories. This produces a circular "map" of emotional space. The three-axis model used in this brief extends Russell's model by adding a third dimension (attentional focus: narrow vs. diffuse), and splits negative valence into aversive and attached subtypes. The extension is the author's synthesis, not an established model.

→ *Referenced when discussing: the three-axis emotion map, the distinction between anxiety and fear, the emotion table.*

---

### Ironic Process Theory
**Originator:** Daniel Wegner (1987)
**Used in:** Chapter 3

Wegner demonstrated that trying to suppress a thought (his famous example: "don't think of a white bear") paradoxically increases its frequency. The theory proposes two processes: one that actively suppresses the thought, and another that monitors whether the suppression is working — and the monitoring process, ironically, keeps the thought accessible. Well replicated and directly relevant to understanding intrusive thoughts and rumination.

→ *Referenced when discussing: "if you shout at the worms, they shout back," thought suppression increasing intrusive thoughts.*

---

### Treisman's Poison Hypothesis
**Originator:** Michel Treisman (1977)
**Used in:** Chapter 3

A hypothesis (not established fact) that nausea in response to internal disturbance — including motion sickness — evolved as a defense against poisoning. The logic: if the brain detects an unexpected mismatch in internal signals, one possible explanation is that a toxin was ingested, and vomiting is the safest response. This remains speculative but is widely cited as a plausible evolutionary explanation.

→ *Referenced when discussing: stress-induced nausea, motion sickness.*

---

### Expectancy-Value Theory / Temporal Motivation Theory
**Originators:** Victor Vroom (expectancy-value theory, 1964); Piers Steel (Temporal Motivation Theory, 2007)
**Used in:** Chapter 4

Vroom proposed that motivation is the product of *expectancy* (belief you can succeed) and *value* (how much you want the reward), divided by *cost*. Steel extended this by adding *delay* as a denominator term — rewards far in the future are automatically discounted. Steel's formulation is: Motivation = (Expectancy × Value) / (Impulsiveness × Delay). The version in this brief substitutes "Effort" for "Impulsiveness" as a more intuitive label, but the structure is Steel's. Temporal discounting is one of the most robust findings in behavioral economics.

→ *Referenced when discussing: the motivation equation, why procrastination happens, the Impossible Task, why deadlines work.*

---

### Incentive Salience Theory (Wanting vs. Liking)
**Originators:** Kent Berridge & Terry Robinson (1990s–present)
**Used in:** Chapter 4

Berridge and Robinson demonstrated in animal studies that the brain's "wanting" system (dopamine-driven, energizing, action-oriented) and "liking" system (opioid-driven, pleasurable, consummatory) are neurologically distinct and can be separated. This explains phenomena like addiction (intense wanting without liking) and has profound implications for understanding motivation, habit, and craving.

→ *Referenced when discussing: wanting vs. liking, why addicts pursue substances that no longer bring pleasure, the motivation paradox ("I want to exercise but I don't want to exercise").*

---

### Negativity Bias / Loss Aversion
**Originators:** Roy Baumeister et al. (negativity bias, 2001); Daniel Kahneman & Amos Tversky (loss aversion / prospect theory, 1979)
**Used in:** Chapter 4

Baumeister's review paper "Bad is stronger than good" documented the pervasive finding that negative events, emotions, and information carry more psychological weight than equivalently positive ones. Kahneman and Tversky's prospect theory formalized a related finding in decision-making: people weigh potential losses roughly twice as heavily as equivalent gains. Together, these findings explain why the brain is biased toward caution, avoidance, and threat-detection.

→ *Referenced when discussing: why bad outcomes loom larger than good ones, the deck stacked toward inaction, why it's hard to take risks.*

---

### Reward Prediction Error
**Originator:** Wolfram Schultz (1990s)
**Used in:** Chapter 4

Schultz's electrophysiology work in monkeys demonstrated that dopamine neurons don't fire in response to rewards themselves — they fire in response to *unexpected* rewards, or to cues that *predict* reward. When a predicted reward is received, there's no dopamine signal (the prediction was correct). When a predicted reward fails to arrive, dopamine activity *decreases* (negative prediction error). This mechanism is now understood to be fundamental to learning, motivation, and the subjective experience of anticipation.

→ *Referenced when discussing: dopamine's role in motivation, why anticipation feels better than the reward itself, reward prediction error.*

---

### Deakin and Graeff's Serotonin Model
**Originators:** J.F.W. Deakin & F.G. Graeff (1991)
**Used in:** Chapter 4

A model proposing that serotonin's primary role is in behavioral inhibition during aversive conditions — helping the organism hold still, wait, and endure discomfort rather than act impulsively. This is one of many serotonin models (the system is extraordinarily complex, with 14+ receptor subtypes), but it captures an important dimension of serotonin function that complements the dopamine-as-action-driver picture.

→ *Referenced when discussing: serotonin's inhibitory role in motivation, the complexity of serotonin function.*

---

### Social Pain Overlap Theory
**Originators:** Naomi Eisenberger, Matthew Lieberman, & Kipling Williams (2003)
**Used in:** Chapter 3

fMRI research showing that social exclusion activates brain regions (particularly the dorsal anterior cingulate cortex) also implicated in physical pain. The finding was influential in establishing that "hurt feelings" are not merely metaphorical — social and physical pain share neural substrates. Subsequent meta-analyses (notably Woo et al., 2014) have suggested the overlap may be less specific than initially claimed, but the core finding — that social pain is neurally real — has held up.

→ *Referenced when discussing: why social rejection stings like physical pain, co-option of ancient systems for modern experience.*
