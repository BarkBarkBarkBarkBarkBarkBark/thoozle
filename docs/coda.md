## Coda — How We Got Here

### From Worms to Worries

Movement is what separates animals from plants. Plants are rooted; they take what the environment gives them. Animals move — toward resources, away from threats. This required a new kind of coordination.

The earliest nervous systems were simple: detect something, move toward it or away from it. A single cell can sense light, temperature, and chemicals using receptors on its surface. But as animals got larger and more complex, they needed a way to coordinate movement across a body much bigger than a single cell. Neurons evolved to carry signals rapidly across distance, allowing a detection at one end of the body to trigger a movement at the other.

For a long time, that was enough. Detect. Approach or avoid. Simple animals didn't need to *understand* their environment — they just needed to react to it.

But selective pressures kept pushing. Animals that could *remember* the environment had an advantage over those that could only react to it. Animals that could *predict* had an advantage over those that could only remember. And animals that could *imagine* — simulate scenarios that hadn't happened yet — had the biggest advantage of all.

Each of these capabilities required more neural machinery. The brain grew. It layered. The old systems didn't go away — they got wrapped in new ones. The hindbrain still runs your heartbeat, just as it did hundreds of millions of years ago. The midbrain still tags things as good or bad. The cortex, the newest addition, just added the ability to *think about* what you're detecting, feeling, and doing.

### The Symbolic Leap

The really strange thing happened when brains got complex enough to manipulate symbols — abstract representations that stand in for real things. A word is a symbol. A number is a symbol. A promise, a grudge, a national border, a stock price — all symbols.

Once the brain could process symbols, it could run its detect-predict-act loop on things that didn't physically exist. You could feel threatened by an *idea*. You could be motivated by a *goal* that wouldn't be achieved for years. You could feel pleasure from *imagining* something that might never happen.

The old emotional machinery — built for real, physical, immediate events — was now being driven by abstractions. And it responds to them with the same hormones, the same muscle tension, the same urgency it would bring to a charging predator. This is why you can lie awake at night, heart pounding, because of something that *might* happen next *month*.

The brain is a prediction engine that outgrew its original job description. It was built to keep an animal alive in the present moment, and it ended up capable of simulating entire lives that haven't been lived yet. This is humanity's greatest gift and most persistent source of suffering.

A final caveat: the machinery described in this brief operates on broadly similar principles across humans, but *not identically*. Genetics, early development, hormonal environment, neurodiversity, and life experience all shape the specific tuning of these systems. Some brains run hotter threat-detection systems; some have more or less efficient dopamine signaling; some process sensory information in atypical ways. The principles are general; the parameters are individual.

Understanding the machine won't make the suffering stop. But it might help you take it a little less personally. The anxiety isn't *you*. The impossible task isn't a moral failure. The voice in your head isn't a separate entity with its own agenda.

It's just worms, doing their job.

Don't shout at them. It only makes them stronger.
