## Glossary

Terms are listed in the order they become relevant, not alphabetically — so you can read straight through if you like.

| Term | Plain-Language Definition |
|------|--------------------------|
| **Neuron** | A brain cell. Long, thin, electrically active. Communicates with other neurons by releasing chemicals. The basic unit of the nervous system. |
| **Neurotransmitter** | A chemical messenger released by one neuron to signal another. Different neurotransmitters carry different kinds of messages (excite, inhibit, modulate). |
| **Receptor** | A protein on the surface of a cell, shaped to catch a specific molecule. When the molecule fits, the cell's behavior changes. Think of it as a lock that only a certain key can open. |
| **Thalamus (Mind Gate)** | A structure deep in the brain that acts as a gateway for sensory information. Almost all sensory input passes through here on its way to the cortex. It filters, prioritizes, and routes. |
| **Cortex (Thoozle)** | The outer layer of the brain — the wrinkly part. Handles perception, language, planning, imagination, and abstract thought. The newest part, evolutionarily. |
| **Primary Cortex** | The first cortical area to receive sensory input. Identifies basic features: edges, frequencies, textures. |
| **Associative Cortex** | Cortical areas that put perception in context by linking it to stored knowledge and related concepts. |
| **Amygdala** | A small structure in the medial temporal lobe that performs rapid threat assessment. Can trigger a physical response before you're consciously aware of what you've perceived. |
| **Hippocampus (Memory Palace)** | A structure critical for forming new memories and organizing stored information. Works closely with the cortex and amygdala. |
| **Predictive Processing** | The theory that the brain constantly generates predictions about incoming sensory information and updates its model based on mismatches (prediction errors). |
| **Prediction Error** | The difference between what the brain expected and what actually happened. A fundamental learning signal. |
| **Affect** | The basic dimension of feeling: positive (good) or negative (bad). The simplest emotional tag. |
| **Valence** | Whether an experience is positive or negative. Often used interchangeably with affect. |
| **Arousal** | The level of physiological activation associated with an emotional state. High arousal = energized; low arousal = calm or depleted. |
| **Negativity Bias** | The brain's tendency to weigh negative outcomes more heavily than equivalently positive ones. Bad looms larger than good. |
| **Dopamine** | A neurotransmitter involved in motivation, reward prediction, and motor activity. Drives the "wanting" aspect of motivation. |
| **Serotonin** | A neurotransmitter involved in mood regulation, behavioral inhibition, and the perceived salience of experience. |
| **Endorphins** | The brain's natural painkillers. Released during pain, exercise, laughter, and social bonding. Produce pleasure and raise the pain threshold. |
| **GABA** | The brain's primary inhibitory neurotransmitter. Calms neural activity. Though most neurons are excitatory, the inhibitory minority (using GABA) plays a critical regulatory role. |
| **Glutamate** | The brain's primary excitatory neurotransmitter. Involved in learning, memory, and general neural communication. |
| **Anandamide** | A naturally occurring cannabinoid. Released after exercise. Promotes rest, appetite, and mild euphoria. |
| **Acetylcholine** | A neurotransmitter involved in muscle contraction, attention, and memory. |
| **Agonist** | A molecule that activates a receptor by mimicking the natural neurotransmitter. |
| **Antagonist** | A molecule that blocks a receptor without activating it, preventing the natural neurotransmitter from binding. |
| **Reuptake Inhibitor** | A molecule that prevents a neurotransmitter from being reabsorbed after release, increasing its availability and duration of action. |
| **Incentive Motivation** | The drive to obtain something rewarding. Pulls you toward action. |
| **Aversive Motivation** | The drive to avoid something harmful or unpleasant. Pushes you away from action (or toward escape). |
| **The Impossible Task** | A task for which the brain's cost-benefit analysis returns an unfavorable result — high perceived effort relative to perceived reward — making initiation feel impossible despite conscious desire. |
| **Temporal Discounting** | The brain's tendency to devalue rewards that are far in the future. A reward available now is worth more to the brain than the same reward available later. |
| **Interoception** | The brain's sense of what's happening inside the body — heart rate, gut tension, breathing rhythm, temperature. Increasingly understood as a foundation of emotional experience. |
| **Sensory Modality** | A distinct channel of sensory information defined by its receptor type and dedicated neural pathway. Vision, hearing, and pain are separate modalities; humans have at least twenty-two. |
| **Proprioception** | The sense of where your limbs are in space, driven by receptors in muscles, tendons, and joints. Why you can touch your nose with your eyes closed. |
| **Nociceptor** | A receptor cell specialized for detecting tissue damage or potentially damaging stimuli. The dedicated hardware of pain — a separate channel from touch. |
| **Cortical Map (Topography)** | The spatial organization of a primary sensory area, in which neighboring neurons respond to neighboring stimuli. The visual map (retinotopy), the frequency map (tonotopy), and the body map (homunculus) are examples. |
| **Hallucination** | Perception without external input — seeing, hearing, or feeling something generated entirely by the brain's own activity. Distinguished from imagination by its involuntariness and perceptual vividness. |
| **Deafferentation** | Loss of sensory input to a brain area, typically due to receptor damage (e.g., retinal degeneration). The affected cortex often becomes hyperexcitable in response. |
| **Excitation/Inhibition (E/I) Balance** | The dynamic equilibrium between excitatory signals (mainly glutamate) and inhibitory signals (mainly GABA) in neural circuits. Disruption of this balance is implicated in hallucinations, seizures, and other conditions. |
| **Charles Bonnet Syndrome** | A condition in which people experiencing vision loss develop vivid visual hallucinations — not from psychiatric illness, but from hyperexcitability in a visual cortex deprived of input. |
| **Sympathetic Nervous System** | The "fight or flight" branch of the autonomic nervous system. Activates the body for action: increases heart rate, dilates pupils, diverts blood to muscles. |
| **Parasympathetic Nervous System** | The "rest and digest" branch of the autonomic nervous system. Calms the body down after a threat has passed. |
| **Prefrontal Cortex** | The front-most region of the cortex. Handles executive functions: planning, impulse control, decision-making, and overriding habitual responses. |
| **Tolerance** | The process by which the brain reduces its sensitivity to a substance after repeated exposure, requiring more of the substance to achieve the same effect. |
| **Dependence** | A state in which the brain has recalibrated around the presence of a substance, such that its absence produces withdrawal symptoms. |
| **Downregulation** | The brain's response to overstimulation of a receptor system: reducing the number or sensitivity of receptors. Part of how tolerance develops. |
