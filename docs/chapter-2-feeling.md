## Chapter 2 — Feeling: The Tag on Every Experience

### What Emotions Actually Are

Emotions feel personal and mysterious — like weather inside your chest. But at the biological level, they're surprisingly mechanical. An emotion is a **coordinated physical response** that biases the brain and body toward a particular kind of action.

When a dog snarls at you, your body doesn't wait for a committee meeting. Your **sympathetic nervous system** — the body's "fight or flight" branch — kicks in instantly. Your heart rate spikes. Your muscles tense. Blood flow shifts away from digestion and toward your limbs. Adrenaline floods your system. All of this happens before you've consciously registered *fear* — because the amygdala's emergency exit already fired.

One influential theory — rooted in the James-Lange tradition and updated by Antonio Damasio's *somatic marker hypothesis* — holds that the conscious feeling of fear is, in some sense, your brain's after-the-fact interpretation of what your body is already doing (see [Theoretical Frameworks](/theoretical-frameworks/)). The brain reads its own body's state — a process called **interoception** — and constructs the feeling from those signals. (Not all researchers agree; Lisa Feldman Barrett's *constructionist theory* argues emotions are assembled from more basic ingredients rather than triggered as fixed programs. But the physical-response-first sequence described here is well documented.)

When the threat passes, the **parasympathetic nervous system** — the "rest and digest" branch — gradually brings the body back to baseline. The two branches work in opposition, like a gas pedal and a brake, and their balance shapes your moment-to-moment emotional tone.

In a non-human animal, this system is straightforward: the stress response prepares the body to fight or run. In humans, the fighting still sometimes happens, but more often the response gets transmuted into words — either an attack ("What did you just say to me?") or a defense ("I didn't do anything!"). The body prepared for physical combat; the cortex translated it into an argument.

### The Three-Axis Model

There are many models of emotion, and no complete consensus. The framework used here adapts and extends James Russell's *circumplex model* (1980), which maps emotions along two dimensions — valence and arousal — by adding a third dimension: attentional focus (see [Theoretical Frameworks](/theoretical-frameworks/)). This three-axis model organizes emotions as follows:

**1. Affect — Is it good or bad?**

This is the most fundamental dimension. Every emotional state has a *valence*: positive (feels good), or negative (feels bad). But negative emotions split into two important subcategories:

- **Aversive** — Something bad is *present* (pain, threat, disgust).
- **Attached** — Something good is *absent* (loss, longing, envy).

This distinction matters because aversive and attached emotions drive different behaviors. Aversion makes you pull away. Attachment makes you reach for what's missing.

**2. Arousal — How activated are you?**

Some emotions are high-energy (rage, excitement, panic). Others are low-energy (calm, grief, contentment). Arousal is the engine speed — how much metabolic fuel is being burned.

**3. Attention — How focused are you?**

Some emotional states narrow your focus (disgust at a specific thing, enthusiasm for a specific goal). Others are diffuse and unfocused (free-floating anxiety, general euphoria).

Combining these three axes produces a map of emotional experience:

| | **Good** | **Bad — Aversive** | **Bad — Absent** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Focused + Active** | Enthusiasm | Anger / Fear | Jealousy |
| **Diffuse + Active** | Euphoria | Panic | Resentment |
| **Focused + Quiet** | Savoring | Disgust | Grief |
| **Diffuse + Quiet** | Calm | Anxiety | Despair |

This isn't the final word on emotions — no model is. But it's useful because it reveals that states which feel very different (like panic and resentment) share structural features (both are active and negative), while states that seem similar (like anxiety and fear) differ in important ways (anxiety is unfocused; fear has a target).

### Emotions Are Physical

This is worth emphasizing: emotions are not just things you feel in your mind. They are events in your body. Stress can cause muscle tension, flushing, restlessness, and nausea. (The nausea, incidentally, may be an evolutionary artifact. One hypothesis, proposed by Michel Treisman in 1977, suggests that the body interprets extreme internal disturbance as possible poisoning and prepares to purge. Motion sickness may work the same way: the mismatch between expected and perceived motion triggers the "maybe we ate something bad" protocol. This remains a hypothesis, not established fact, but it's a plausible and widely cited one.)

Every subjective emotional state has a corresponding hormonal and physiological profile. Happiness involves different chemicals and body states than sadness. Anger involves different ones than grief. The brain's interoceptive system — introduced above — is constantly monitoring these body states and feeding them back into the emotional picture. The brain didn't evolve a separate "feelings module" — it co-opted the body's existing chemical signaling systems to create rapid, full-body behavioral biases.

### Relevance and the Worms That Won't Shut Up

Remember the worms from Chapter 1? Some of them work in the Memory Palace, and their job is to track relevance. When something gets tagged as important — very good or very bad — a worm gets assigned to it. That worm's job is to bring it up periodically, to make sure you don't forget.

This is useful when the important thing is an ongoing threat (don't forget there's a predator near the water hole) or an ongoing opportunity (the berry bushes are ripe this week). It is less useful when the important thing is an embarrassing comment you made at a party in 2014.

Obsessive thoughts and intrusive memories are, in this framework, worms that won't chill. They've been assigned to something that feels important, and they keep doing their job — loudly, repeatedly, whether you want them to or not.

Here's the critical thing: **if you shout at the worms, they shout back.** Fighting an intrusive thought signals to the brain that the thought is important (you're expending energy on it, after all), which strengthens the association, which makes the worm louder. The research on this is well established — Daniel Wegner's work on *ironic process theory* (1987) demonstrated that thought suppression tends to increase the frequency of the suppressed thought (see [Theoretical Frameworks](/theoretical-frameworks/)).

Don't shout at the worms. It only makes them stronger.

### Human Complications

In simple animals, the emotional system is tightly coupled to survival: threats trigger avoidance, resources trigger approach. Humans complicated things enormously by developing the capacity for abstract thought. We can now derive pleasure or pain from events that have nothing to do with physical survival — a social slight, a philosophical disagreement, imagined future catastrophes.

These abstract emotional responses appear to be running on the same ancient hardware. Influential fMRI work by Naomi Eisenberger and colleagues (2003) found that social rejection activates some of the same brain regions as physical pain — though later meta-analyses have suggested the overlap may be less specific than initially reported. The thrill of a stock market gain uses the same reward pathways as finding food. The old systems were co-opted to make sense of an increasingly symbolic environment — which is clever, but it means your body sometimes prepares for a fistfight when someone disagrees with you on the internet.

Human rumination makes it worse. Animals experience a threat, respond, and move on. Humans experience a threat, respond, and then *replay the event thousands of times in their imagination*, each replay triggering a fresh (if diminished) emotional response. The prediction engine, left unsupervised, becomes a suffering engine.
